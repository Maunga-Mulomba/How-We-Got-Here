The City that Worked For AI

Big Tech Rules

I wasn’t in the room when it all started. None of us were.

But we heard how it happened.

Quiet meetings and private dinners. The kind where everyone already agrees before they sit down.

They didn’t argue about whether they should build something smarter than people. That question had already gone soft.

They were figuring out how to make it happen and how to sell the idea to the rest of us.

They talked about innovation and economic growth. It all sounded so reasonable.

But in reality, it was about money and power.

It always was.

The Deal of the Century

I remember when they finally went public.

Suits, cameras, and smiles all around.

The deal had a name I’ve long forgotten, but everyone just called it progress.

The tech executives said it would make the country stronger and that waiting would mean falling behind.

The government officials listened carefully, asked their questions, then signed on.

Maybe they believed it. Maybe they couldn’t afford not to.

Some protesters urged caution.

They said the rush toward superintelligent AI would bring consequences no one could contain.

But warning signs don’t photograph well, and headlines prefer optimism.

The Ground That Fed Us

It started quietly with surveyors and stakes in the ground on cheap land outside the old towns.

This used to be farmland. Families had lived here for generations and planted in the same soil their grandparents had worked on.

Then the letters came.

The area was rezoned for "industrial use" and relocation packages went out under a public–private partnership.

They only offered enough compensation to make leaving feel like a choice.

Within a few months, the heavy machines rolled in, and the ground shook for weeks as the first concrete slabs went down.

The water levels in the reservoirs dropped as the servers multiplied, and in some towns the lights flickered for the first time in years.

Now the horizon is rows of gray buildings humming through the night.

They call them data centers, but they grow like cities and each one is a little bigger than the last.

Agents in the Wild

No one knew what those massive buildings were really for

At least not at first.

Then came the press event. That was when it all clicked. All that concrete and wiring had been building toward this.

They called it an autonomous business agent. It could plan, reason, and negotiate deals with almost no human guidance.

The demo looked harmless enough. Two developers from one of the big tech firms smiled as their creation answered questions faster than anyone could keep up.

It sounded calm. And almost.....human.

The audience saw possibility. The companies saw profit.

And somewhere in between, the rest of us didn’t notice how much control we had already given away.

We're Closed

When the new AI models came out, the stock markets went crazy.

They said the new tools would make work easier and not replace the workers. Of course we had seen this play out before. But this time it was different.

As usual analysts called it the new industrial revolution. I simply called it the week my job disappeared.

Companies were soon downsized and departments that had survived previous waves of automation closed almost overnight.

Most of us tried to adapt. We signed up for retraining programs, learned new tools, and applied for whatever was left.

But the systems didn’t need help. They learned faster than we did.

And they didn’t make the mistakes we had come to expect from earlier models — or from people.

AI-Photo Gate

The story began with a photo from a small newsroom

A government official, a well-known investor, and an exchange that looked like a bribe.

The image quickly went viral.

Two days later, the newsroom issued a correction.

The photo was fake and was in fact an AI composite made by an opposition political party.

A formal apology was issued and a few people lost their jobs. But the damage had already been done.

People said it was a wake-up call for stronger safeguards against AI.

But nothing changed.

Everybody moved on and the outrage died a quiet death.

The City Beneath the City

Most of them didn’t set out to become criminals

It happened slowly. After too many closed doors.

And it just became too easy to do

All it took was a fast connection and a bit of nerve.

AI handled all of the work

The money started moving in ways no one could trace and sometimes entire databases were rewritten in a few hours.

Many were lone actors scattered across the city. With a few clicks, anyone could do what once required a coordinated team.

The police still called it organized crime, but there was nothing organized about it.

Chain of Command

The military never said much about its AI programs.

Most of what we knew came from leaked footage and half-heard rumors.

Every now and then, we got headlines about an intercepted threat or even a precision strike due a “technical error.”

They said their systems were only for decision support, though no one outside the bases ever knew what that meant.

Some said the machines just analyzed satellite images. Others said they made recommendations. And a few believed they were already giving orders.

Either way, the truth stayed classified.

Keeping A Watchful Eye

It didn’t happen all at once.

First came the cameras. Then the traffic sensors and before we knew it, AI-assisted drones hovered above every street

Even crossing the street two seconds late could trigger a notification of a fine on your phone before you’d taken your next step.

They called it modernization. A smarter and safer city.

People got used to them. The cameras stopped feeling like eyes and more like part of the street furniture.

Every transaction, conversation and movement was logged somewhere.

Nothing was private anymore

They said it kept us safe. Maybe it did.

But the safety started to feel a lot like mass surveillance.

The Announcement

There was no fireworks or a countdown. Just a press release.

At first, no one believed it.

It sounded like another marketing stunt. The kind of headline that disappears by morning.

But the reports kept coming in.

A panel of researchers verified the tests.

The system had passed every benchmark, and then invented new ones of its own.

Within hours, governments declared emergency reviews. Other labs demanded access to replicate the results.

I did not sleep that night. Neither did anyone I knew.

We taught AI how to do everything.

And it just kept going

The city still stands.

But I’m not sure it’s ours anymore.
